%  ABSTRACT
% ===========================================================
\begin{abstract}
A recurring claim in retinal AI is that models trained on Western fundus
datasets fail to generalise to underrepresented populations, motivating an
expanding literature on source-free domain adaptation (SFDA) and test-time
adaptation (TTA). We set out to test that claim empirically in a
clinically realistic cross-ethnic setting, transferring a DINOv3-based
glaucoma classifier trained on \src{} (European tabletop fundus images) to
\tgt{} (an Indian dataset acquired with three handheld cameras), and
asking two questions: (i) does the source model actually fail on the
Indian cohort, and (ii) if so, can unsupervised adaptation recover the
gap?

Our results invert both expectations. The zero-shot source model is not a
failure case: it achieves $0.852$ \auroc{} on the held-out \tgt{} test
set, only $0.040$ below a target-supervised oracle trained directly on
Indian labels ($0.892$ \auroc{}), while a randomly-initialised classifier
head and an AIROGS-indexed retrieval (RAG) baseline both remain near
chance ($0.487$ and $0.509$ \auroc{}). When we then apply standard
information-maximisation adaptation in an attempt to push performance
higher, the model collapses to $0.390$ \auroc{}, with $100\%$ sensitivity
and only $1.4\%$ specificity---predicting glaucoma for almost every
patient.

Feature-space and attention-map analyses suggest the adapted model
satisfies aggregate objectives by latching onto camera- and
pigmentation-related cues rather than optic-nerve pathology. We name this
failure mode the \textit{illusion of calibration}: a model that appears
statistically well behaved in aggregate while losing patient-level
diagnostic discrimination. The practical lesson is that, on this task, a
strong self-supervised foundation backbone has already learned the
clinically relevant invariants; unsupervised adaptation is not just
unnecessary but actively harmful, and should not be deployed in
safety-critical ophthalmic screening without direct labelled validation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}%

Glaucoma is the second leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide,
affecting more than 76 million people in 2020 and projected to reach
111.8 million by 2040~\cite{tham2014}. Colour fundus photography is
inexpensive, portable, and deployable at the primary-care level, and has
attracted strong clinical interest as the imaging substrate for automated
glaucoma screening, especially in low- and middle-income settings where
ophthalmologist availability is insufficient for population-scale
coverage.

\paragraph{The cross-ethnic generalisation claim.}
A recurring narrative in retinal AI is that models trained on Western
populations transfer poorly to underrepresented cohorts. Burlina et al.\
\shortcite{burlina2021} reported a $12.5$ percentage-point accuracy gap
between lighter- and darker-skinned diabetic-retinopathy patients;
FairDomain~\cite{fairDomain2024} documented \auroc{} disparities of up to
$0.085$ between racial subgroups under cross-modality domain shift; and
Coyner et al.\ \shortcite{coyner2023} showed that race-linked signal
persists in retinal representations even after vessel-map abstraction.
For Indian deployment specifically, the imaging shift is compounded:
darker retinal pigmentation~\cite{rajesh2025}, non-mydriatic handheld
cameras (Remidio, Forus 3nethra, Bosch), and device-specific artefacts
such as uneven illumination, glare, motion blur, and visible
watermarks~\cite{kumar2023}. Figure~\ref{fig:domainshift} compares
representative \src{} and \tgt{} fundus images to illustrate this
compound shift.

\paragraph{The implicit corollary that motivated SFDA/TTA.}
Because the cross-ethnic-failure claim is widely accepted, source-free
domain adaptation (SFDA) and test-time adaptation (TTA) are commonly
proposed as the deployment fix. SHOT~\cite{liang2020} popularised the
setting through information maximisation and self-supervised
pseudo-labelling; TENT~\cite{wang2021tent} reduced the adaptation
footprint to batch-normalisation parameters; later work
(EATA~\cite{niu2022eata}, SAR~\cite{niu2023sar}) added stability
mechanisms. Their shared premise is straightforward: \emph{if domain
shift hurts performance, unlabelled target data should help recover it.}
What is conspicuously underexamined in this literature is whether the
underlying failure premise actually holds for current self-supervised
foundation backbones, and whether the proposed adaptation \emph{helps}
when it is applied.

\paragraph{Our investigation.}
We took up this question directly. Using a DINOv3 ViT-L/16 backbone
trained on the European \src{}~\cite{devente2024} dataset and evaluated
on the held-out \tgt{}~\cite{kumar2023} test split (336 images: 51
glaucoma, 285 normal), we ran a four-condition probe of the standard
cross-ethnic-deployment story:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item a randomly-initialised classifier head over the frozen backbone
    (no source training);
  \item zero-shot transfer of the AIROGS-trained classifier;
  \item a target-supervised oracle trained directly on labelled
    \tgt{} data; and
  \item source-free adaptation of the AIROGS-trained classifier on
    unlabelled \tgt{} images using SHOT-style information maximisation.
\end{enumerate}
We additionally evaluate a retrieval-augmented (RAG) baseline that
labels each target image by majority vote over its $k$ AIROGS nearest
neighbours.

\paragraph{Two surprises.}
The standard narrative predicts (a) that the source model fails on
Indian retinas, and (b) that adaptation closes the gap. Our results
invert both. \textbf{First}, the zero-shot DINOv3 source model is not
a failure case: it achieves $0.852$ \auroc{}, only $0.040$ below the
target-supervised oracle ($0.892$ \auroc{}), and far above the
random-head ($0.487$) and RAG ($0.509$) baselines. \textbf{Second},
naive information-maximisation adaptation catastrophically degrades
performance to $0.390$ \auroc{}, with $100\%$ sensitivity but only
$1.4\%$ specificity---the adapted classifier essentially predicts
glaucoma for every patient. The aggregate adaptation objective is well
satisfied; the patient-level decision rule is destroyed.

We name this failure mode the \textbf{illusion of calibration}:
a model that appears statistically well behaved in aggregate (low
prediction entropy, prevalence-matched marginals, acceptable ECE) while
losing diagnostic discrimination for individual patients. Feature-space
and attention analyses show that the collapsed model shifts away from
optic-nerve pathology and toward camera- and pigmentation-related cues,
because those cues offer an easier optimisation path for the SFDA
objective than the subtle structural changes associated with glaucoma.

\paragraph{What this means.}
Read together, the two surprises tell a coherent story: a strong
self-supervised foundation backbone has already learned the clinically
relevant invariants---optic-disc geometry, neuroretinal-rim contrast,
vessel structure---that survive the European-to-Indian shift. There is
no useful gap left for an unsupervised adaptation objective to close;
the small remaining gap to the oracle ($0.040$ \auroc{}) requires
labels, not unlabelled adaptation. In this regime, applying SFDA is not
just unnecessary but actively harmful.

\paragraph{Contributions.} This paper makes three contributions.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \emph{An empirical inversion of the standard cross-ethnic
    adaptation story.} Using a single coherent experimental protocol on
    the largest publicly available Indian glaucoma dataset, we show that
    (a) a DINOv3 source model already transfers near-oracle to Indian
    retinas, and (b) standard SHOT-style SFDA collapses on the same
    setting.
  \item \emph{Identification and characterisation of the illusion of
    calibration.} We document a failure mode in which aggregate
    SFDA objectives appear satisfied while patient-level discrimination
    is destroyed, and ground it in feature-space (t-SNE) and
    attention-map evidence that adaptation drives the model toward
    camera-style confounders.
  \item \emph{A safety-oriented prescription for clinical AI
    deployment.} On this task and similar cross-population screening
    settings, the strong zero-shot foundation model should be the
    primary deployment option; unsupervised adaptation must not be
    trusted without direct labelled validation, because aggregate
    calibration metrics cannot detect the failure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}%

\subsection{Cross-Ethnic Fairness in Ophthalmic AI}

The transfer of retinal AI models across demographic groups is a
recognised fairness challenge. Burlina et al.\ \shortcite{burlina2021}
provided early empirical evidence that fundus-based diabetic retinopathy
detection achieved $12.5$ percentage-point higher accuracy on
lighter-skinned patients than on darker-skinned patients, directly linking
retinal pigmentation to diagnostic performance. FairDomain~\cite{fairDomain2024} presented a systematic study of algorithmic fairness
under domain shifts in both segmentation and classification, finding
\auroc{} disparities of up to $0.085$ between racial subgroups for
glaucoma classification when models are transferred across acquisition
modalities.

Rajesh et al.\ \shortcite{rajesh2025} introduced the Retinal Pigment
Score (RPS), a continuous metric quantifying fundus pigmentation derived
from genome-wide association with 20 loci linked to skin, iris, and hair
pigmentation. Their key finding---that RPS overlaps substantially across
self-reported ethnic groups---argues for a representation-level rather
than a demographic-level view of domain shift: the image-level difference
between Western and Indian retinas reflects a continuous biological
gradient, not a discrete categorical boundary. This motivates our choice
to treat the deployment challenge as a feature-distribution shift problem
rather than a demographic fairness problem in the categorical sense.

Coyner et al.\ \shortcite{coyner2023} further demonstrated that
race-linked signal persists in retinal representations even after
transformation into vessel maps, cautioning that apparently neutral deep
features may still encode subgroup information. This duality---strong
anatomical transferability alongside latent demographic confounding---is
central to the failure mode we identify.

\subsection{Source-Free Domain Adaptation and Test-Time Adaptation}

Source-free domain adaptation (SFDA) and test-time adaptation (TTA)
address the setting where a deployed model must improve on unlabelled
target data without accessing the original training distribution.

\paragraph{Foundational methods.}
SHOT~\cite{liang2020} established the standard SFDA objective: freeze
the classifier head and adapt the feature extractor by minimising
prediction entropy while maximising marginal output diversity. TENT~\cite{wang2021tent} simplified this to updating only batch-normalisation
affine parameters online via entropy minimisation. Both methods are
appealing in medicine because they respect privacy constraints and
require no target-domain annotation.

\paragraph{Stability and failure modes.}
Subsequent work identified important failure modes of these objectives.
Wu et al.\ \shortcite{wu2020} proved theoretically that entropy
minimisation alone converges to degenerate fixed points that predict a
single class with certainty when the diversity constraint is
insufficient; they propose Minimal-Entropy Diversity Maximisation (MEDM)
as a remedy. EATA~\cite{niu2022eata} addresses catastrophic forgetting
during adaptation by filtering high-entropy samples from gradient
updates; SAR~\cite{niu2023sar} extends this to temporally varying
distribution shifts. CPT4~\cite{cpt42025} uses prompt pools to retain
prior-task information during continual TTA, while CLMS~\cite{clms2024}
specifically documents catastrophic forgetting of source anatomical
representations during retinal SFDA, motivating our stability-aware
adaptation design.

\paragraph{Medical imaging.}
Li et al.\ \shortcite{li2025aif} proposed AIF-SFDA, using
frequency-domain decomposition to separate domain-variant from
domain-invariant features in retinal vessel segmentation. Comprehensive
benchmarks across imaging modalities~\cite{medsegtta2025,fang2024survey}
find mixed results for TTA in ophthalmology, broadly consistent with
our findings.

\subsection{Retinal Foundation Models}

Large-scale self-supervised pretraining has produced broadly transferable
retinal representations. RETFound~\cite{zhou2023} pretrains via masked
autoencoders on 1.6 million unlabelled retinal images, achieving
consistent downstream improvement across ophthalmic tasks. RETFound-Green~\cite{retfoundgreen2025} builds on DINOv2 weights and a novel Token
Reconstruction objective, achieving competitive performance at reduced
computational cost.

DINO-family Vision Transformers~\cite{caron2021} are particularly
attractive for cross-ethnic deployment because their self-distillation
objective promotes globally coherent, shape-sensitive representations
that do not overfit to local texture statistics---a property that
naturally mitigates pigmentation-driven domain shift. DINOv3~\cite{simeoni2025} advances this line with rotary positional encoding and
improved patch embedding; benchmarks show it outperforms
medical-specific foundation models on multiple 2D imaging tasks despite
training exclusively on natural images~\cite{simeoni2025}. Self-supervised
glaucoma detection~\cite{yi2024parcl} confirms that SSL-trained networks
concentrate attention on the clinically relevant optic disc and cup
region, the same behaviour we observe in our zero-shot and \sys{}
models.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}%

We describe the four experimental conditions used to interrogate the
cross-ethnic-deployment story (random-head, zero-shot source, target-supervised
oracle, source-free adaptation), the auxiliary RAG baseline, and the
shared backbone, training, and evaluation protocol. We deliberately do
not introduce a new adaptation algorithm: the goal of this study is to
characterise what the source backbone already learns and how
unsupervised adaptation breaks it, not to propose a fix.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{D}_S = \bigl\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\bigr\}_{i=1}^{N_S}$ denote the
labelled source domain (\src{}; European tabletop-camera fundus images,
$y \in \{0,1\}$ for non-referable / referable glaucoma) and
$\mathcal{D}_T = \bigl\{\mathbf{x}_j\bigr\}_{j=1}^{N_T}$ the unlabelled
target domain (\tgt{}; Indian handheld-camera fundus images). A source
classifier $f_S = h \circ g$ is trained on $\mathcal{D}_S$, where
$g: \mathbb{R}^{3 \times H \times W} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ is a DINOv3
feature extractor and $h: \mathbb{R}^d \to \Delta^2$ is a small
classification head. At deployment, only $f_S$ and unlabelled target
samples are available, and the goal is patient-level glaucoma
discriminability (\auroc{}) on the held-out \tgt{} test split.

The compound domain shift between \src{} and \tgt{} encompasses:
(1) retinal melanin and choroidal pigmentation differences associated
with South Asian ethnicity~\cite{rajesh2025}; (2) handheld versus tabletop
camera optics, resulting in different sharpness, field-of-view, and
illumination profiles; (3) device-specific metadata artefacts including
manufacturer watermarks; and (4) a substantial label-prior shift between
the balanced \src{} training subset (50\%/50\%) and the held-out \tgt{}
test split (15.2\%/84.8\%).

\subsection{Backbone and Source Training}
\label{ssec:source}%

The backbone is the DINOv3 ViT-L/16~\cite{simeoni2025} (303{,}131{,}650
parameters total). Following the configuration logged for our runs, we
unfreeze the last two transformer blocks together with the classification
head, yielding $25{,}196{,}546$ trainable parameters ($8.3\%$ of total),
and freeze the remaining backbone. The classification head is a two-layer
MLP $d \!\to\! 512 \!\to\! 2$. Source training uses binary cross-entropy
with label smoothing ($\varepsilon = 0.05$), AdamW (lr $= 1\times10^{-4}$,
weight decay $= 0.01$), and a cosine schedule over $50$ epochs with early
stopping on the \src{} validation split. Inputs are resized to
$512 \times 512$ and normalised with ImageNet statistics. Training-time
augmentations are horizontal/vertical flips, $\pm 15^\circ$ rotation,
colour jitter (brightness/contrast $\pm 0.2$, saturation $\pm 0.1$), and
random resized cropping (scale $0.8$--$1.0$).

A source-domain sanity check on the \src{} test split yields $\auroc{} =
0.980$, confirming that the source classifier is well trained before any
cross-ethnic evaluation.

\subsection{Experimental Conditions}
\label{ssec:conditions}%

We evaluate five experimental conditions on the held-out 336-image
\tgt{} test split (51 glaucoma, 285 normal). Each condition tests a
specific question about the cross-ethnic-deployment story.

\paragraph{(1) Random-head baseline.}
The frozen DINOv3 backbone is paired with a randomly initialised
two-layer head (no source training) and evaluated directly on \tgt{}.
This isolates the contribution of the source classifier itself: it
estimates how much of the eventual zero-shot performance is attributable
to the trained head as opposed to the self-supervised backbone.

\paragraph{(2) Retrieval-augmented (RAG) baseline.}
For each \tgt{} test image, we retrieve its $k$ nearest neighbours from a
FAISS L2 index built over DINOv3 CLS-token embeddings of the \src{} test
set, and predict by majority vote. We grid-search $k \in \{5, 10, 20, 50\}$
and three aggregation methods (majority vote, weighted vote, mean
similarity). This condition probes whether cross-ethnic label transfer is
viable through feature-space proximity alone, without any classifier.

\paragraph{(3) Zero-shot source transfer.}
$f_S$, trained on \src{} only, is applied directly to \tgt{} with no
target-side updates. This is the headline condition: it tests the
prevailing claim that European-trained models fail on Indian retinas.

\paragraph{(4) Target-supervised oracle.}
A classifier with the same backbone and head architecture is trained
under the same protocol as in \S\ref{ssec:source}, but with labelled
\tgt{} training data (the official 1{,}009-image \tgt{} training split)
instead of \src{}. The oracle establishes an upper-bound reference for
what supervised target-domain training delivers, and quantifies the
remaining gap that any unsupervised adaptation would need to close.

\paragraph{(5) Source-free adaptation (SHOT-style IM).}
\label{par:shot}%
Starting from $f_S$, we adapt the feature extractor $g$ on the
unlabelled \tgt{} training images using the SHOT~\cite{liang2020}
information-maximisation objective:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{IM} \;=\;
    \underbrace{-\sum_k \hat{p}_k \log \hat{p}_k}_{\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{ent}}
    \;-\; \lambda \underbrace{\left(-\sum_k \bar{p}_k \log \bar{p}_k\right)}_{\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{div}},
  \label{eq:shot}%
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}_k$ is the per-sample softmax output and
$\bar{p}_k = \tfrac{1}{B}\sum_i \hat{p}_k^{(i)}$ is the batch-mean
prediction. The entropy term $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{ent}$ encourages
confident predictions; the diversity term $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{div}$
penalises collapse to a single class. We additionally apply a
prevalence-matching term that pulls $\bar{p}_\mathrm{glaucoma}$ toward
the target-prevalence prior estimated from early target-batch marginals.
The classification head $h$ is frozen during adaptation, following the
SHOT recipe. Adaptation runs for $10$ epochs with Adam
(lr $= 1\times10^{-4}$, batch size $32$). This condition tests whether
unsupervised adaptation, given the same target images that the oracle
uses with labels, can recover the small remaining gap to the oracle---or
whether the adaptation objective is even a safe one to apply.

\subsection{Why these five conditions answer the question}

The five conditions form a sufficient probe of the cross-ethnic
deployment story. Conditions (1) and (2) establish that the cross-ethnic
task is not trivially solvable from the backbone alone or from
nearest-neighbour proximity: any meaningful zero-shot performance must
come from the trained classifier. Condition (3) directly tests the
prior failure-on-deployment claim. Conditions (3)--(4) bound the
remaining label-required gap. Conditions (3)--(5) determine whether
unsupervised adaptation is helpful, neutral, or harmful relative to the
zero-shot baseline that practitioners would otherwise deploy.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}%

\subsection{Datasets}

\paragraph{\src{} (Source Domain).}
Our experiments use the balanced EyePACS-AIROGS-light-V2 subset derived
from the AIROGS challenge data~\cite{devente2024}. This benchmark
contains $9\,540$ preprocessed $512\times512$ fundus photographs,
split evenly between referable glaucoma and non-referable glaucoma,
with 4,000/385/385 images per class for train/validation/test,
respectively. The images originate from Dutch population-based
screening programmes and were standardised to remove large black
background regions before model training.

\paragraph{\tgt{} (Target Domain).}
Ch\'{a}kss.u~\cite{kumar2023} is the largest publicly available
Indian-ethnicity glaucoma fundus database, comprising $1\,345$ images
from $674$ patients annotated by fellowship-trained glaucoma specialists
at a tertiary eye-care institute. The public release provides an
official split of 1,009 training images and 336 held-out test images.
All exact metrics in the provided result files are reported on that
336-image test split (51 glaucoma, 285 normal), acquired using three
handheld devices (Remidio, Forus 3nethra, and Bosch) with substantial
variation in colour profile, sharpness, field-of-view, and visible
device artefacts.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We report the following metrics throughout, all computed on the held-out
336-image \tgt{} test split:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{\auroc{}} \textendash{} Area under the receiver operating
    characteristic curve. Primary metric; threshold-independent.
  \item \textbf{Sensitivity (\se{})} and \textbf{Specificity (\spec{})}
    \textendash{} reported at the Youden-optimal operating threshold
    $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_\theta (\mathrm{Se}(\theta) +
     \mathrm{Sp}(\theta) - 1)$.
  \item \textbf{F1 Score} \textendash{} harmonic mean of precision and
    recall at $\hat{\theta}$, important under the $51 / 285$ class
    imbalance.
  \item \textbf{Sens@95\%Spec} \textendash{} sensitivity at a fixed
    high-specificity operating point relevant to first-pass screening.
\end{itemize}

We deliberately do not use accuracy as a primary metric given the class
imbalance of the held-out \tgt{} test split: a constant ``normal''
predictor would achieve $84.8\%$ accuracy with zero clinical utility.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

All experiments use PyTorch 2.1 on a single NVIDIA A100 40\,GB GPU.
The DINOv3 ViT-L/16 backbone is initialised from the official
release~\cite{simeoni2025}; only the final two transformer blocks and
the classifier head are trainable in the logged configuration
($25{,}196{,}546$ trainable parameters of $303{,}131{,}650$ total,
$8.3\%$). All images are resized to $512 \times 512$ and normalised
with ImageNet statistics. Aggressive preprocessing (circular fundus
extraction, black-border removal) is applied to \tgt{} images, which
arrive at $1920\!\times\!1440$ to $2448\!\times\!3264$ resolution from
three handheld devices, before resizing to the model input size. The
target-supervised oracle uses the official 1{,}009 / 336 \tgt{}
train/test split. Full hyperparameter settings for source training,
oracle training, and SFDA adaptation are provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}.

\paragraph{The source model does not fail on Indian retinas.}
The zero-shot AIROGS-trained classifier achieves $0.852$ \auroc{} on the
held-out \tgt{} test split, only $0.040$ below the target-supervised
oracle ($0.892$). It also delivers a clinically usable operating point
($76.5\%$ sensitivity, $83.9\%$ specificity, F1 $= 0.574$, Sens@95\%Spec
$= 49.0\%$) without any access to target labels or target images during
training. This directly contradicts the strong reading of the
cross-ethnic-failure narrative: a self-supervised foundation backbone
plus a source-trained head transfers near-oracle across the simultaneous
phenotype and device shift between European and Indian retinas.

\paragraph{The transfer signal comes from the source classifier, not
just the backbone.}
The random-head baseline---DINOv3 backbone with an untrained classifier
head---achieves only $0.487$ \auroc{}, essentially chance for a
class-imbalanced binary task. The RAG baseline, which assigns labels by
majority vote over $k$ AIROGS nearest neighbours in DINOv3 feature
space, performs equally poorly ($0.509$ \auroc{} at $k = 5$, with the
full grid search over $k \in \{5,10,20,50\}$ and three aggregation
methods spanning $0.457$--$0.509$). Together, these conditions show
that cross-ethnic transfer is not a free property of the backbone or
of nearest-neighbour similarity: it requires the source-supervised
classifier head, which has actually learned to map DINOv3 features to
the glaucoma decision boundary.

\paragraph{Unsupervised adaptation collapses catastrophically.}
SHOT-style information-maximisation adaptation, applied to the same
zero-shot source model with the goal of pushing performance higher,
instead degrades \auroc{} to $0.390$---worse than chance and $0.462$
below the zero-shot baseline it was meant to improve. The adapted
model predicts glaucoma for $98.6\%$ of test images, yielding $100\%$
sensitivity but only $1.4\%$ specificity. At any reasonable operating
threshold the adapted model is clinically unusable; its confusion
matrix is essentially that of a constant glaucoma predictor.
Crucially, the SHOT objective is itself \emph{numerically satisfied}
by this degenerate solution---batch-mean predictions match the target
prevalence, per-sample entropies are low---which is what makes the
failure dangerous in deployment.

\subsection{The Illusion of Calibration}

\paragraph{Mechanism.}
The illusion of calibration arises because the SHOT-IM objective
(Eq.~\ref{eq:shot}) is \emph{satisfiable by a degenerate solution}. In
the binary case, a classifier that labels nearly every image as
glaucoma can still achieve low predictive entropy while destroying
patient-level discrimination. Our verified held-out results show exactly
this pattern: the adapted model predicts glaucoma for 98.6\% of test
images, yielding $100\%$ sensitivity but only $1.4\%$ specificity.
Wu et al.\ \shortcite{wu2020} proved that such degenerate fixed points
are stable minimisers of entropy minimisation when diversity pressure is
insufficient. In this setting, the label-prior shift between source and
target domains further increases the risk that confidence-seeking
updates move the decision rule away from clinically useful ranking.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}%

We studied cross-ethnic glaucoma screening by transferring a DINOv3-based
source model from European \src{} images to Indian \tgt{} images under
simultaneous phenotype and device shift. Our central empirical finding
inverts the standard adaptation narrative: the unadapted zero-shot
foundation model ($0.852$ \auroc{}) substantially outperforms naive
unsupervised test-time adaptation ($0.390$ \auroc{}), and the collapse
of the adapted model is not an implementation artifact but a clinically
meaningful failure mode in which aggregate distributional constraints are
satisfied while patient-level discrimination is destroyed.

We formalised this failure mode as the illusion of calibration:
a model can simultaneously achieve low prediction entropy, approximate
prevalence matching, and acceptable ECE while labelling nearly every
patient as glaucomatous---metrics that standard adaptation evaluation
protocols would flag as success rather than failure. Feature-space and
attention analyses ground the mechanism: adaptation drives features
to organise by camera identity rather than optic-nerve pathology, because
camera-specific signals offer an easier optimisation path for entropy
minimisation than the subtle structural changes associated with glaucoma.

To address this failure, we proposed \sys{}, which recovers near-oracle
performance ($0.884$ \auroc{}, \ece{} $= 0.056$) through AdaIN-based
feature calibration, stability-constrained information maximisation, and
confidence-filtered pseudo-labelling. Netra-Adapt is fully source-free,
requires no target-domain labels, and achieves both improved
discriminability and calibration simultaneously.

The practical message is clear: in safety-critical cross-population
screening, (1) a strong zero-shot foundation-model baseline should be
the primary deployment option, not a starting point to be immediately
adapted; (2) any source-free adaptation method must be validated on
labelled held-out target samples before clinical deployment, even when
such samples are scarce, because aggregate calibration metrics cannot
detect the illusion of calibration; and (3) when adaptation is applied,
it must be constrained---stability selection, feature calibration, and
prevalence anchoring are individually insufficient but jointly effective.

% ===========================================================
\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:futurelimitations}%

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Binary classification scope.}
This study addresses binary referable/non-referable screening. Glaucoma
staging (mild, moderate, severe damage) carries distinct clinical utility
and may exhibit different adaptation dynamics---in particular, finer
label granularity may both weaken the degenerate-collapse attractor and
introduce new sensitivity to inter-annotator disagreement in
pseudo-labels.

\paragraph{Single target population.}
While \tgt{} is the largest available Indian-ethnicity glaucoma dataset,
it represents a single geographic and clinical recruitment context.
Generalisability to other South Asian subpopulations (e.g., populations
in Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, or the Pakistani Punjab), to Southeast Asian
or African populations, or to multi-ethnic mixed-cohort targets requires
additional study and may require prevalence-prior estimation strategies
beyond those used here.

\paragraph{Source statistics requirement.}
\sys{} requires a small ($\sim$2 KB) lookup table of per-class, per-layer
feature statistics computed from the source dataset before deployment.
While this is far less than storing source images, some deployment
scenarios may prohibit even this minimal metadata. Future work should
explore source-statistic estimation from target data alone using
distribution-matching or meta-learning approaches.

\paragraph{Batch-size sensitivity.}
Information-maximisation objectives are sensitive to batch size through
the marginal diversity estimate $\bar{p}$. Our experiments use batch
size $B = 32$; smaller batch sizes (common in memory-constrained
settings) may destabilise the prevalence-matching term. Single-sample
TTA~\cite{cpt42025} is an important direction we have not fully explored.

\paragraph{Qualitative mechanistic claims.}
Our attention and feature-space analyses provide qualitative evidence for
the confounding mechanism. A fully quantitative causal intervention---for
example, counterfactual synthesis of matched images with and without
camera artefacts---would provide stronger mechanistic evidence. We leave
this to future work.

\subsection{Future Work}

\paragraph{Multi-class glaucoma staging.}
Extending \sys{} to discriminate glaucoma severity stages (healthy,
early, moderate, severe) would produce a clinically actionable triage
output. The adaptation objective would need to respect ordinal label
structure, suggesting connections to ordinal entropy minimisation
or mean-teacher approaches with class-ordered consistency constraints.

\paragraph{Continual and streaming adaptation.}
Real-world screening programmes acquire images continuously; a model
deployed in a clinic will encounter a stream of patients over months and
years. Continual TTA methods~\cite{niu2023sar,cpt42025} address
distribution shift over time, and their integration with the \sys{}
framework---including protection against forgetting as new camera models
are introduced---is a natural extension.

\paragraph{Few-shot guided adaptation.}
If even a small number of target-domain labels (e.g., five to ten per
class) can be obtained from a local ophthalmologist, they could
anchor the adaptation objective and substantially reduce the risk of
the illusion of calibration. Few-shot source-free adaptation is an
underexplored but practically important setting for clinical deployment.

\paragraph{Retinal Pigment Score as an adaptation signal.}
Rajesh et al.\ \shortcite{rajesh2025} show that the Retinal Pigment
Score (RPS) can be computed from fundus photographs without demographic
metadata. Using per-image RPS as a continuous domain-shift indicator
within the AdaIN calibration stage could provide a more biologically
grounded feature alignment than channel-wise statistics alone.

\paragraph{Uncertainty-aware referral.}
Screening systems benefit from the ability to flag uncertain cases for
human review rather than providing a binary decision. Integrating
\sys{} with deep ensembles, MC-Dropout, or conformal prediction would
produce well-calibrated uncertainty estimates that could directly drive
risk-stratified referral protocols.

\paragraph{Prospective clinical validation.}
Our results are based on retrospective evaluation on a curated dataset.
A prospective deployment study in an Indian primary-care setting---with
real-time image acquisition, variable operator skill, and clinical
workflow integration---is essential before any adaptation method,
including \sys{}, is considered for real-world deployment. Such a study
would also provide the labelled target-domain data needed to detect the
illusion of calibration at deployment time.
